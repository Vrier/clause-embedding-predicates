# Verb Classes for Clause-Embedding Predicates

This document describes the semantic and syntactic classes of English
clause-embedding predicates in `predicates.csv`. The organisation follows
Levin (1993): each class is characterised by its semantic core, its membership,
and the syntactic alternations its members participate in. Class numbers follow
Levin and VerbNet numbering where an established correspondence exists; new
numbers are assigned for classes not represented in those frameworks.
Cross-references are to `alternations.md`.

---

## Part I: Cognitive Predicates

Cognitive predicates express a propositional attitude of a cognitive agent toward
an embedded proposition. The subject argument is always an animate (typically
human) experiencer and the embedded clause denotes the content of the cognitive
state or event.

---

### 1.1 Comprehend Verbs

**Members of this class:** appreciate, be_aware, be_certain, be_conscious,
be_convinced, be_sure, be_unaware, comprehend, forget, know, remember,
understand, wot.

*(Corresponds to Levin §87.1 / VerbNet comprehend-87.)*

Comprehend verbs express a stative, enduring cognitive relation between an agent
and a propositional content. The subject knows, understands, or is aware of the
fact described by the embedded clause. The embedded proposition is presupposed to
be true: if one knows that *p*, then *p* is the case. These predicates are
therefore factive.

The comprehend verbs are uniformly stative. They do not freely appear in the
progressive, and they resist telic frame adverbials (*\*She was knowing that he
had left*). They differ from the discover verbs (§1.2) in lacking the telic,
punctual character of a change-of-state event.

These verbs are *responsive* predicates: they take both declarative and
interrogative complements (*She knew that he was there / She knew where he was*).
The responsive behaviour follows from their semantics, as knowing a fact and
knowing who/what/where/why are equally well-formed epistemic states.

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1): *She knew [that he was guilty].*
- **Interrogative complement** (§3): *She knew [whether he was guilty] / [who was
  guilty].*
- **NP complement alternation** (§6, partial): *She knew [the answer] / She
  remembered [the fact].*
- **Raising alternation** (§8, *be_certain*, *be_sure* only): *She is certain to
  win.*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): stative — \**She is knowing that he is guilty.*

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Gerund complement: \**She knew having been there.*
- Recipient alternation: \**She knew him that he was guilty.*
- Quotative alternation: \**She knew 'He was there.'*
- Factive passive: \**He was known that he was guilty.*
- Pro-complement alternation: \**She knew so.*

---

### 1.2 Discover Verbs

**Members of this class:** accept, ascertain, catch_on, check, conclude,
cotton_on, deduce, detect, determine, diagnose, discern, discover, divine,
fathom, figure_out, find, find_out, grasp, identify, ignore, infer, intuit,
learn, miss, pick_up, piece_together, realize, recognize, register, rumble,
sink_in, suss_out, take_in, twig, verify, work_out.

*(Corresponds to Levin §87.2 / VerbNet discover-84.)*

Discover verbs describe an achievement event in which a cognitive agent comes to
know or becomes aware of a fact. The complement clause denotes the content of
what is discovered or realised. The transition from a state of not-knowing to a
state of knowing is the core semantic contribution of these predicates. The
embedded proposition is presupposed to be true at the moment of discovery: if one
discovers that *p*, then *p* is the case. These predicates are therefore
semi-factive (the complement is veridical but the presupposition holds at the
result state, not a prior state).

The discover verbs are aspectually distinct from the comprehend verbs (§1.1): as
telic achievements they are compatible with the progressive in an imminence or
anticipatory reading, with *in X time* frame adverbials, and with the punctual
adverb *suddenly*:

> She suddenly realised that he was lying.  
> She found out in an instant that the keys had gone.  
> She is figuring out that the plan won't work.

The majority of discover verbs are *responsive* predicates: they take both
declarative and interrogative complements (*She discovered that the keys were
missing / She discovered who had taken them*). A small subset takes only
declarative complements.

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1): *She discovered [that the safe was empty].*
- **Interrogative complement** (§3, majority): *She found out [who had taken it] /
  [whether the door was locked].*
- **NP complement alternation** (§6, partial): *She discovered [the truth].*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): achievement — *She suddenly realised that he had lied.*

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Factive passive: \**He was discovered that he was lying.*
- Recipient alternation: \**She discovered him that the safe was empty.*
- Quotative alternation: \**She realised 'The keys were gone.'*
- Pro-complement alternation: \**She realised so.*
- Raising alternation: \**He discovered to be absent.*

---

### 1.3 Conjecture Verbs

**Members of this class:** assess, assume, be_confident, be_skeptical,
be_uncertain, believe, calculate, categorize, conceive, conjecture, consider,
contemplate, credit, deem, derive, disbelieve, dismiss, doubt, evaluate, expect,
fancy, figure, foresee, gather, gauge, generalize, guess, hallucinate, investigate,
judge, make_out, measure, muse, ponder, posit, presuppose, reason, reason_out,
reckon, recollect, reconsider, rediscover, ruminate, suppose, surmise, suspect,
theorize, think, trow, trust, visualize, ween.

*(Corresponds to Levin §29.5 / VerbNet conjecture-29.5.)*

Conjecture verbs express a non-factive propositional attitude: the subject takes
a stance toward an embedded proposition without presupposing it to be true. The
embedded proposition is merely entertained, assumed, or held with varying degrees
of epistemic confidence. These predicates are non-factive: neither *She believes
that p* nor *She doubts that p* entails or presupposes *p*.

Conjecture verbs are stative. The progressive is generally unacceptable
(\**She is believing that he is guilty*), and the predicates are not easily
modified by telic or punctual adverbials.

These verbs are *anti-responsive* predicates: they do not take interrogative
complements. One cannot say *\*She believed whether he was there* or *\*She
supposed who was there*. This property distinguishes them sharply from the
comprehend verbs (§1.1) and discover verbs (§1.2), which are responsive.

The subclass of predicates encoding evaluation or judgment (*consider*, *deem*,
*judge*, *find*, *assess*) also takes a small clause complement alternating with
the finite clause (*She considered him a fool / She considered that he was a fool*).

These verbs uniquely participate in the **pro-complement alternation** (§13):
*I think so / I believe so / I suppose so / I reckon so.* This is the single most
diagnostic property of this class.

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1): *She believed [that he was innocent].*
- **Infinitival complement** (§2, partial, ECM): *She believed [him to be innocent].*
- **Small clause complement** (§5, evaluative subset): *She considered [him a fool].*
- **NP complement alternation** (§6, partial): *She doubted [his story].*
- **Pro-complement alternation** (§13): *She believed so / She didn't think so.*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): stative — \**She is believing that he is there.*

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Interrogative complement: \**She believed whether he was there.*
- Raising alternation: \**He believed to be guilty.*
- Factive passive: \**He was believed that he was guilty.*
- Quotative alternation: \**She believed 'He was guilty.'*

---

### 1.4 Imagine Verbs

**Members of this class:** daydream, dream, fantasize, imagine, pretend.

*(No established Levin/VerbNet class; closest is VerbNet consider-29.9.)*

Imagine verbs describe a cognitive agent entertaining an imagined, counterfactual,
or fictive state of affairs. The complement clause denotes content that the subject
conceives of without any commitment — and often with a counter-commitment — to its
truth. These predicates are counter-factive in the strong case (*pretend*: the
subject represents *p* as true while knowing *p* is false) or non-factive in the
weaker case (*dream*, *fantasize*: the subject merely entertains *p*).

These verbs share the property that the embedded proposition is explicitly or
implicitly marked as non-actual. They differ from conjecture verbs (§1.3) in
that the subject's attitude is not one of epistemic assessment but of imaginative
or fictive engagement.

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1, partial): *She imagined [that she was elsewhere].*
- **Infinitival complement** (§2, partial): *She imagined [being elsewhere].*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): stative — \**She is imagining so / \*She imagined so.*

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Interrogative complement: \**She imagined whether she was elsewhere.*
- Recipient alternation: \**She imagined him that she was there.*
- Quotative alternation: \**She imagined 'I was elsewhere.'*
- Pro-complement alternation: \**She imagined so.*
- Raising alternation: \**She dreamed to be elsewhere.*

---

## Part II: Emotive Predicates

Emotive predicates express an affective attitude of an experiencer toward a
propositional content. The subject argument is always an animate experiencer; the
embedded clause denotes the proposition that is the object of the attitude.

---

### 2.1 Regret Verbs (Factive Emotive)

**Members of this class:** abhor, be_annoyed, be_ashamed, be_delighted,
be_disappointed, be_excited, be_glad, be_happy, be_horrified, be_hurt,
be_irritated, be_outraged, be_pleased, be_proud, be_relieved, be_sad,
be_satisfied, be_sorry, be_surprised, be_thankful, be_thrilled, be_upset,
bemoan, bewail, bristle, deplore, despise, detest, dislike, enjoy, envy, exult,
flip_out, freak_out, fuss, gloat, groan, grumble, hate, lament, like, loathe,
love, marvel, mind, mourn, overlook, panic, pout, regret, rejoice, relish, resent,
rue, savour, seethe, wail, welcome.

*(Corresponds to Levin §31.2 subject-experiencer / VerbNet admire-31.2.)*

Regret verbs express an affective or evaluative attitude of a subject experiencer
toward a fact. The subject is an experiencer who has a positive or negative
emotional response to the truth of the embedded proposition. The embedded
proposition is presupposed to be true: if one regrets that *p*, then *p* is the
case. These predicates are therefore factive.

The experiencer is consistently the matrix subject, distinguishing these verbs
from the psych-causative class (§3.1), in which the stimulus is the matrix subject
and the experiencer is the object in active voice. Regret verbs do not form a
factive passive (*\*She was regretted that she had left*), whereas psych-causative
verbs do (*She was surprised that he had left*).

These verbs are stative in their primary reading: one may be glad, regret, love,
or hate a fact without undergoing a change of state. They do not readily occur in
the progressive with a that-clause complement (\**She is regretting that she went*).

Many of these verbs also take a gerund complement, giving an alternation between
the finite clause and the gerund:

> She regretted [that she had lied] / She regretted [lying].  
> She hated [that he had come] / She hated [him coming].

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1): *She regretted [that she had lied].*
- **Gerund complement** (§4, majority): *She regretted [lying].*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): stative — \**She is regretting that she went.*

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Interrogative complement: \**She regretted whether she had lied.*
- Raising alternation: \**She regretted to have lied (as raising).*
- Recipient alternation: \**She regretted him that she had lied.*
- Quotative alternation: generally \**She regretted 'I had lied.'*
- Factive passive: \**She was regretted that she had lied.*
- Pro-complement alternation: \**She regretted so.*

---

### 2.2 Fear Verbs (Non-Factive Emotive)

**Members of this class:** agonize, be_afraid, be_angry, be_furious, be_jealous,
be_mad, be_worried, celebrate, complain, cringe, despair, disdain, disregard,
dread, fear, fret, fume, grieve, moan, pray, scoff, sigh, worry.

*(Corresponds partially to Levin §31.2 non-factive subset.)*

Fear verbs express a prospective, counterfactual, or evaluatively non-committed
affective attitude. Like the regret verbs (§2.1), the subject is an experiencer,
but the embedded proposition is not presupposed to be true. The experiencer fears,
dreads, or worries about a state of affairs that may or may not obtain. These
predicates are non-factive.

The class is distinguished from the regret class primarily by factivity: *She
regretted that he had left* presupposes he left; *She feared that he had left*
does not. Many verbs in this class take propositions that are oriented toward a
possible future (*dread*, *worry*, *fret*).

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1): *She feared [that he would not return].*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): stative — \**She is fearing that he will not come.*

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Interrogative complement: \**She feared whether he would come.*
- Quotative alternation: \**She feared 'He would not come.'*
- Factive passive: \**She was feared that he would not come.*
- Pro-complement alternation: marginal at best (*\*She feared so*).

## Part III: Psych-Causative Predicates

---

### 3.1 Amuse Verbs (Psych Causative)

**Members of this class:** abash, affront, aggravate, agitate, alarm, alienate,
amaze, amuse, anger, anguish, annoy, antagonize, appall, appease, arouse,
astonish, astound, awe, baffle, befuddle, bewilder, boggle, bore, bug, calm,
captivate, chagrin, charm, cheer, chill, comfort, concern, confound, confuse,
content, crush, daunt, daze, dazzle, deject, delight, demoralize, depress,
devastate, disarm, discomfit, disconcert, discourage, disgrace, disgruntle,
disgust, dishearten, disillusion, dismay, dispirit, displease, disquiet,
distress, disturb, dumbfound, dupe, elate, electrify, embarrass, embitter,
embolden, enchant, encourage, enrage, enrapture, entertain, enthrall, enthuse,
entrance, exasperate, excite, exhilarate, fascinate, faze, flabbergast, flatter,
floor, fluster, frighten, frustrate, gall, galvanize, gladden, gratify, haunt,
hearten, horrify, humble, hurt, impress, incense, infuriate, inspire, interest,
intimidate, intrigue, invigorate, irk, irritate, jade, jar, madden, mesmerize,
miff, mortify, move, mystify, nauseate, nettle, nonplus, offend, outrage,
overwhelm, pain, perplex, petrify, please, puzzle, rankle, repel, revolt, rile,
sadden, satisfy, scare, shame, shock, sicken, soothe, spellbind, spook, startle,
stimulate, stun, stupefy, surprise, tantalize, terrify, thrill, tire, torment,
torture, traumatize, trouble, unnerve, unsettle, upset, vex, wow.

*(Corresponds to Levin §31.1 / VerbNet amuse-31.1.)*

Amuse verbs express a causal relation in which a stimulus (the matrix subject)
brings about a psychological state in an experiencer (the matrix object). The
embedded complement clause is always a declarative that-clause describing the
stimulus proposition that causes the experiential state. These verbs are factive:
the stimulus proposition is presupposed to be true (*It surprised me that she had
left* presupposes she left).

Amuse verbs differ structurally from the regret verbs (§2.1) in their argument
structure: the experiencer is the object in active voice and the stimulus is the
subject. This yields the characteristic **extraposition** pattern, in which the
that-clause subject is replaced by expletive *it* and the experiencer appears as
object:

> It surprised/*annoyed/*delighted her [that he had won].

The clausal-subject variant (*That he had won surprised her*) is possible but
stylistically marked. Amuse verbs also participate in the **factive passive**, in
which the experiencer object is promoted to subject and the that-clause is
retained:

> She was surprised/*annoyed/*delighted [that he had won].

This passive is uniquely available to the amuse verbs among non-communicative
clause-embedding predicates.

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1): *It surprised her [that he had come].*
- **Infinitival complement** (§2, majority): *It surprised her [to find him there].*
- **Extraposition** (§7, obligatory in active voice): *It surprised her [that he had come].*
- **Factive passive** (§12): *She was surprised [that he had come].*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): stative in experiencer-passive reading; eventive in active.

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Interrogative complement: \**It surprised her whether he had come.*
- Raising alternation: \**She surprised to have come.*
- Recipient alternation: \**It surprised her him that he had come.*
- Quotative alternation: \**She surprised 'He had come.'*
- Pro-complement alternation: \**It surprised her so.*

## Part IV: Communicative Predicates

Communicative predicates take a propositional complement that denotes a message
conveyed, asserted, implied, or directed by a speaking agent. The subject is
always an agent and the embedded clause denotes the content of the speech act.

---

### 4.1 Say Verbs (Assertive Report)

**Members of this class:** add, address, adjudge, admonish, advertise, affirm,
agree, allege, announce, answer, argue, articulate, assert, asseverate, attest,
authorize, aver, avow, babble, bark, beam, bellow, bet, bicker, bitch, blog,
blurt_out, boast, brag, bring_up, broadcast, cackle, certify, challenge, chant,
charge, chatter, chide, choke_out, chronicle, circulate, claim, clarify, comment,
communicate, compute, concede, confess, contend, contest, convey, corroborate,
crow, cry, debate, deceive, declaim, declare, decree, denounce, deny, depict,
describe, dictate, disagree, discuss, dispel, display, dispute, divulge, drawl,
educate, elaborate, email, embellish, emphasize, endorse, ensure, envision,
establish, estimate, exclaim, exhibit, explain, expose, express, fax, forecast,
foretell, gab, get_around, get_out, giggle, go_around, gossip, grant, growl,
grunt, guarantee, gush, hint, hiss, hold, holler, hoot, howl, hush_up, imply,
indicate, indict, insinuate, insist, intercept, interject, intimate, jest, joke,
lay_out, leak, lecture, log, maintain, mark, mention, moralize, mumble, murmur,
mutter, narrate, negotiate, note, object, obsess, omit, opine, ordain, pester,
petition, phone, picture, pinpoint, plead, post, praise, preach, predict,
prejudge, presume, proclaim, profess, prophesy, propose, protest, publicize,
publish, put_about, put_across, quarrel, question, quip, quote, radio, rant,
rationalize, rave, read, reaffirm, reassert, recall, recant, recap, recommend,
record, reiterate, reject, relate, remark, reminisce, repeat, reply, report,
represent, repress, research, respond, restate, retort, rule, say, scream,
scribble, select, shout, showcase, shriek, signify, simulate, sing, sketch,
snap, snarl, snitch, snort, sob, specify, spell_out, splutter, spout, squeal,
stammer, state, stipulate, stutter, submit, suggest, summarize, tease, test,
testify, thunder, tweet, type, uncover, underestimate, underline, underscore,
update, uphold, utter, venture, videotape, voice, volunteer, vote, wager, warrant,
whimper, whisper, will, write, yell.

*(Corresponds to Levin §37.7 and related subclasses / VerbNet say-37.7,
manner_speaking-37.3, lecture-37.11, complain-37.8, chit_chat-37.6.)*

Say verbs form the largest class in this inventory. They share the ability to
take a finite declarative complement describing the propositional content of an
assertion or utterance. The subject is a speaking agent and the embedded clause
describes the content of what is asserted, claimed, reported, or stated. These
predicates are non-factive: saying or claiming that *p* does not presuppose *p*.

The class contains several recognisable subgroups defined by further manner or
illocutionary distinctions: manner-of-speaking verbs (*whisper*, *shout*,
*mutter*, *growl*, *bark*), assertion verbs (*assert*, *claim*, *declare*,
*maintain*, *contend*), reporting verbs (*report*, *note*, *mention*,
*describe*, *chronicle*), and verbs of opinion expression (*suggest*, *hint*,
*imply*, *intimate*). Despite internal variation, all share the core complement
pattern and non-factive semantics.

The majority of say verbs participate in the **quotative alternation**, taking a
directly quoted string in place of the that-clause. This property distinguishes
the entire communicative domain from all non-communicative clause-embedding
predicates (cognitive, emotive, psych-causative).

A subset takes the mandative subjunctive complement (*insist*, *stipulate*,
*decree*, *ordain*, *recommend*, *propose*, *suggest*), indicating a directive or
preferential rather than purely assertive speech act.

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1): *She said [that he was late].*
- **Quotative alternation** (§10, majority): *She said ['He is late.'].*
- **NP complement alternation** (§6, partial): *She announced [the results].*
- **Mandative subjunctive** (§11, partial): *She insisted [that he leave].*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): stative — \**She is saying so in an ongoing state.*

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Interrogative complement (majority): \**She said whether he was late.*
- Recipient alternation (majority): \**She said him that he was late.*
- Raising alternation: \**She seemed to have said.*
- Factive passive: \**He was said that he was late (as experiencer passive).*
- Pro-complement alternation: \**She said so (as pro-complement substitution).*

---

### 4.2 Tell Verbs (Report-To)

**Members of this class:** advise, alert, assure, caution, confide, convince,
demonstrate, forewarn, get_across, get_through, implore, instruct, persuade,
pledge, promise, reassure, remind, signal, swear, tell, threaten, tip_off, vow,
warn.

*(Corresponds to Levin §37.1.1 / VerbNet tell-37.2, advise-37.9.)*

Tell verbs describe the transfer of a message from a speaking agent to a
recipient. They are syntactically distinguished from the say verbs (§4.1) by the
presence of a recipient argument between the verb and the embedded complement:

> She told him [that the meeting was cancelled].  
> They warned the driver [that the bridge was unsafe].

For many verbs in this class, the recipient argument is obligatory:

> \*She informed that the meeting was cancelled.  
> She informed them that the meeting was cancelled.

The recipient argument may be demoted to a *to*-PP in some cases, giving a
structural parallel with ditransitive verbs of transfer (*tell someone about X*).
The content argument may alternatively be expressed as an infinitival complement
(*She told him to leave / She warned him to stay back*), creating an overlap with
directive predicates (§6.1).

The most diagnostic alternation is the **factive passive**, in which the recipient
object is promoted to matrix subject:

> He was told [that the meeting was cancelled].  
> We were warned [that the bridge was unsafe].

This passive distinguishes the tell verbs from the say verbs (§4.1), which
express no recipient and therefore cannot form a recipient-passive.

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1): *She told him [that the project had failed].*
- **Recipient alternation** (§9, defining): *She told him [that the project had failed].*
- **Infinitival complement** (§2, majority): *She told him [to leave].*
- **Factive passive** (§12, majority): *He was told [that the project had failed].*
- **Quotative alternation** (§10, partial): *She promised ['I will return.'].*
- **Mandative subjunctive** (§11, partial): *She urged [that he leave].*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): stative.

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Interrogative complement (majority): \**She told him whether he was late.*
- Raising alternation: \**She seemed to tell.*
- Pro-complement alternation: \**She told so.*

---

### 4.3 Confess Verbs (Concessive)

**Members of this class:** acknowledge, admit, apologize, approve, bear_out,
blame, bless, bring_home, chastise, come_clean, conceal, confirm, congratulate,
console, consult, contact, depose, detail, disappoint, disapprove, disclose,
dissatisfy, document, fess_up, flaunt, forgive, gasp, give_away, grimace,
inform, insult, let_on, let_out, let_slip, notify, own, own_up, pity, point_out,
prove, relieve, reveal, rouse, share, show, stress, weep, whine.

*(Corresponds to Levin §37.10 / VerbNet confess-37.10.)*

Confess verbs describe a communicative act in which the speaker acknowledges the
truth of a proposition. Unlike the say verbs (§4.1), these predicates are factive:
if one admits that *p*, confesses that *p*, or reveals that *p*, then *p* is
presupposed to be true. The class thus occupies the intersection of communicative
and factive predicates.

The term *concessive* reflects the most typical pragmatic profile of these verbs:
the speaker's acknowledgment of the truth of *p* is often presented as a
concession, disclosure, or confirmation that the proposition — which may have been
previously unknown, doubted, or contested — is indeed the case. Verbs of
inadvertent disclosure (*let_on*, *let_slip*, *give_away*) are included in this
class because they also carry a factive presupposition and describe an act of
(unintentional) communication.

Some verbs in the class also participate in the gerund complement alternation:

> She admitted [that she had lied] / She admitted [lying].  
> She acknowledged [that she had been wrong] / She acknowledged [being wrong].

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1): *She admitted [that she had been wrong].*
- **Gerund complement** (§4, partial): *She admitted [being wrong].*
- **NP complement alternation** (§6, partial): *She admitted [the error].*
- **Quotative alternation** (§10, partial): *She admitted ['I was wrong.'].*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): stative.

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Interrogative complement (majority): \**She admitted whether she had been wrong.*
- Raising alternation: \**She admitted to have been wrong (as raising).*
- Pro-complement alternation: \**She admitted so.*
- Factive passive (majority): \**She was admitted that she had been wrong.*

---

### 4.4 Lie Verbs (Counter-Factive)

**Members of this class:** delude, fabricate, fake, feign, fool, lie, manufacture,
misinform, mislead, retract, trick.

*(No established Levin class; closest to VerbNet say-37.7 counter-factive subset.)*

Lie verbs describe a communicative act in which the speaker or agent presents a
proposition as true while it is false, or retracts a previously asserted
proposition. These predicates are counter-factive or anti-veridical: *She lied
that he was innocent* or *She misled them that the project had succeeded* both
presuppose the falsity of the complement proposition.

The class is small and relatively heterogeneous. Some members (*retract*) involve
withdrawal of a previously asserted proposition rather than a primary false
assertion. Others (*feign*, *fake*) describe the enactment of a false appearance
rather than a propositional lie. What unites the class is the negation of the
embedded proposition in the commitment space of the speaker.

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1, partial): *She lied [that he had been there].*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): stative.

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Interrogative complement: \**She lied whether he had been there.*
- Raising alternation: \**She lied to have been there.*
- Recipient alternation: generally absent.
- Pro-complement alternation: \**She lied so.*
- Factive passive: \**She was lied that he had been there.*

## Part V: Desiderative Predicates

---

### 5.1 Want Verbs (Desiderative)

**Members of this class:** anticipate, consent, decide, desire, hope, intend,
plan, prefer, resolve, want, wish.

*(Corresponds to Levin §32.1 / VerbNet want-32.1.)*

Want verbs express the desiderative or volitional attitude of an agent toward a
state of affairs. The subject desires, intends, or prefers that a proposition
hold. The embedded proposition is not presupposed to be true; indeed, for most
desiderative predicates the embedded state of affairs is prospective and
hypothetical at the time of embedding. These predicates are non-factive.

The defining syntactic property of want verbs is the subject-control infinitive:
the infinitival complement is obligatory (all members take *comp_inf*), and in
the canonical case the understood subject of the infinitive is controlled by the
matrix subject (*She wanted to leave*). The finite that-clause complement is
available for many members (*She wanted that she would leave*; *She hoped that he
would come*) but is restricted or archaic for others (*\*She intends that she goes*
in most varieties of English).

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1, majority): *She hoped [that he would come].*
- **Infinitival complement** (§2, all members, subject-control): *She wanted [to leave].*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): stative — \**She is wanting to leave (in most varieties).*

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Interrogative complement (majority): \**She wanted whether he would come.*
- Raising alternation: \**He wanted to be late (as raising).*
- Factive passive: \**She was wanted that she would leave.*
- Quotative alternation: \**She wanted 'He will come.'*
- Pro-complement alternation: marginal (*I hope so*; \**I want so*).

## Part VI: Directive Predicates

---

### 6.1 Order Verbs (Directive)

**Members of this class:** allow, ask, beg, command, demand, direct, enjoin,
exhort, force, mandate, order, permit, prescribe, request, require, urge.

*(Corresponds to Levin §§58–67 / VerbNet order-60.1, ask-58.3, beg-58.2,
force-59, allow-64, urge-58.1.)*

Order verbs describe a directive speech act or act of authority in which the
matrix subject attempts to bring about an action by the complement-clause subject.
The directive predicates encode a range of illocutionary force from polite request
(*ask*, *beg*, *request*) through strong directive (*order*, *command*, *require*)
to permissive or prohibitive acts (*allow*, *permit*). They are non-factive: no
directive predicate presupposes that the directed action occurs.

The defining syntactic diagnostic for this class is the **mandative subjunctive
complement**: the embedded verb appears in the bare (non-finite) form rather than
with agreement inflection:

> She ordered [that he **leave** immediately].  
> They required [that the report **be** submitted by Friday].  
> She urged [that the committee **consider** the proposal].

The mandative subjunctive marks the complement as encoding a directive content
rather than a factual or epistemic claim. The infinitival complement is also
characteristic (*She ordered him to leave / She asked him to go*).

The that-clause complement with indicative is generally possible only in the
counter-factual reading (*She demanded that he was innocent* — stating a demand
about a factual matter) or in historical/legal registers.

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Infinitival complement** (§2, majority): *She ordered him [to leave].*
- **Mandative subjunctive** (§11, majority): *She required [that the form **be**
  completed].*
- **Declarative complement** (§1, partial): *She demanded [that he was present].*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): stative.

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Interrogative complement (majority): \**She ordered whether he would leave.*
- Raising alternation: \**She ordered to leave.*
- Factive passive: \**She was ordered that she would leave (as experiencer passive).*
- Pro-complement alternation: \**She demanded so.*

## Part VII: Raising-Evidential Predicates

---

### 7.1 Seem Verbs (Raising Evidential)

**Members of this class:** appear, emerge, follow, mean, seem, transpire,
turn_out.

*(Corresponds to Levin §109 / VerbNet seem-109.)*

Seem verbs are raising predicates that express an evidential relation: the
predicate conveys the speaker's or narrator's epistemic stance toward an embedded
proposition, typically based on perceptual, inferential, or textual evidence.
These predicates are non-factive: *It seems that p* does not entail *p*.

Seem verbs are uniquely defined in this inventory by the **raising alternation**:
the subject of the finite complement can be raised to become the matrix subject,
with the embedded predicate reduced to an infinitive:

> It seems [that he is ill].  →  He seems [to be ill].  
> It turned out [that she was wrong].  →  She turned out [to be wrong].

This alternation is exclusively available to these predicates among all
clause-embedding predicates in this inventory. The impossibility of raising with
cognitive or emotive predicates (*\*He knew to be there / \*She regretted to have
lied*) is a sharp diagnostic for the raising-evidential class.

The matrix subject in the finite construction is invariably expletive *it*,
confirming that the predicate assigns no semantic role to a matrix-subject argument.
Expletive and idiom-chunk subjects are freely available under raising, further
confirming the absence of a selectional restriction on the raised argument:

> There seems to be a problem.  
> The cat seems to be out of the bag.

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1): *It seems [that he is ill].*
- **Raising alternation** (§8, defining): *He seems [to be ill].*
- **Extraposition** (§7, obligatory): *It seems / \*That he is ill seems.*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): stative.

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Interrogative complement: \**It seems whether he is ill.*
- Recipient alternation: \**It seemed him that she was wrong.*
- Quotative alternation: \**She seemed 'He is ill.'*
- Factive passive: \**He was seemed that he was there.*
- Pro-complement alternation: \**It seemed so.*

## Part VIII: Perception Predicates

---

### 8.1 See Verbs (Perception)

**Members of this class:** feel, hear, notice, observe, overhear, perceive, see,
sense, spot, watch, witness.

*(Corresponds to Levin §§30–30.4 / VerbNet see-30.1, hear-30.2.)*

See verbs describe the direct perceptual awareness of a state of affairs by an
experiencer. The subject argument is a perceiver and the embedded clause denotes
the content of what is perceived. These predicates are semi-factive in their
declarative-complement use: *She saw that he was injured* presupposes that he was
injured, as direct perception provides a veridical basis for the embedded
proposition.

Perception predicates are the only class in this inventory that takes both a
that-clause and a small clause complement freely:

> She saw [that he crossed the road].  *(finite — conscious awareness)*  
> She saw [him cross the road].  *(small clause — direct perception event)*  
> She saw [him crossing the road].  *(progressive small clause — ongoing event)*

The small clause complement describes a directly perceived event, while the finite
complement describes an interpreted fact. All members are *responsive* predicates,
taking both declarative and interrogative complements:

> She noticed [that he was nervous].  
> She noticed [how nervous he was] / [whether he was nervous].

Some members (*notice*, *spot*, *overhear*, *witness*) have a clearly telic,
achievement character; others (*watch*, *observe*) are more activity-like or
stative. The class is therefore aspectually heterogeneous.

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Declarative complement** (§1, majority): *She noticed [that he was nervous].*
- **Interrogative complement** (§3, all members): *She noticed [whether he was nervous].*
- **Small clause complement** (§5): *She saw [him cross the road].*
- **NP complement alternation** (§6, partial): *She heard [the news].*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): mixed — achievement for *notice*, *spot*,
  *overhear*, *witness*; stative for *watch*, *observe*, *sense*.

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Raising alternation: \**She noticed to be nervous.*
- Recipient alternation: \**She noticed him that he was nervous.*
- Quotative alternation: \**She saw 'He was nervous.'*
- Factive passive: \**He was seen that he was nervous (as experiencer passive).*
- Pro-complement alternation: \**She noticed so.*

## Part IX: Implicative Predicates

---

### 9.1 Manage Verbs (Implicative)

**Members of this class:** bother, care, fail, happen, manage, try.

*(Corresponds to Levin §§95–96 / VerbNet succeed-74, try-61.)*

Manage verbs are *implicative* predicates: the truth value of the embedded
complement can be inferred from the truth or falsity of the matrix predication.
Positive implicatives (*manage*, *happen*) entail the truth of their complement:
*She managed to leave* entails *she left*. Negative implicatives (*fail*) entail
the falsity of their complement: *She failed to leave* entails *she did not leave*.
Try-type predicates (*try*, *bother*, *care*) are non-implicative in that the
complement's truth value is not directly entailed.

The defining syntactic property of implicative predicates is the infinitival
complement, which is obligatory. The finite that-clause complement is marginal
or absent for most members of the class:

> She managed [to escape].  *(grammatical)*  
> ??She managed [that she would escape].  *(marginal)*

As aspect achievements or activities, implicatives are compatible with the
progressive and with telic frame adverbials, distinguishing them from the stative
predicates throughout the rest of this inventory.

These verbs participate in the following alternations:

- **Infinitival complement** (§2, defining): *She managed [to escape].*
- **Stative/achievement** (§14): achievement — *She is trying to escape.*

These verbs do not participate in the following alternations:

- Interrogative complement (majority): \**She managed whether she would escape.*
- Recipient alternation: \**She managed him to escape.*
- Quotative alternation: \**She managed 'I escaped.'*
- Factive passive: \**She was managed to escape.*
- Pro-complement alternation: \**She managed so.*
